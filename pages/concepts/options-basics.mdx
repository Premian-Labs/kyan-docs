# Options Basics

Options are derivative contracts that give you the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a predetermined price before or on a specific date. They are one of the most versatile instruments in finance, allowing traders to hedge risk, speculate on price direction, or generate income from existing positions.

## What is an option?

Every option contract has two sides: a buyer and a seller (also called the writer). The buyer pays a premium upfront for the right the contract grants. The seller collects that premium and takes on the obligation to fulfill the contract if the buyer exercises it.

There are two types of options:

- **Call options** give the buyer the right to _buy_ the underlying asset at the strike price. Traders buy calls when they expect the price to go up.
- **Put options** give the buyer the right to _sell_ the underlying asset at the strike price. Traders buy puts when they expect the price to go down.

The **strike price** is the predetermined price at which the option can be exercised. The **premium** is the cost of purchasing the option. And every option has an **expiration date**, after which the contract is no longer valid.

Whether an option has value at expiration depends on the relationship between the strike price and the current market price of the underlying asset.

## Key terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Call | Right to buy the underlying asset at the strike price |
| Put | Right to sell the underlying asset at the strike price |
| Strike price | The predetermined price at which the option can be exercised |
| Premium | The cost of buying an option contract |
| Expiration | The date when the option contract expires |
| In-the-money (ITM) | Option has intrinsic value (e.g., a call where the market price is above the strike) |
| Out-of-the-money (OTM) | Option has no intrinsic value (e.g., a call where the market price is below the strike) |
| At-the-money (ATM) | The strike price equals the current market price of the underlying |

## The Greeks

Options prices are influenced by several factors beyond the current price of the underlying asset. These sensitivities are measured by a set of values known as the Greeks.

- **Delta** measures how much the option price changes for a $1 move in the underlying. A call with a delta of 0.50 will gain roughly $0.50 for every $1 the underlying rises.
- **Gamma** measures the rate of change of delta itself. High gamma means delta shifts quickly as the underlying moves, which is common for at-the-money options near expiration.
- **Theta** measures time decay, the amount of value the option loses each day as it approaches expiration. All else being equal, options lose value over time.
- **Vega** measures sensitivity to implied volatility. When volatility rises, option premiums increase, and vega tells you by how much.

Understanding the Greeks is essential for managing options positions, especially when constructing multi-leg strategies or hedging a portfolio.

## Options on Kyan

Kyan's options have several characteristics that distinguish them from traditional options markets:

- **Cash-settled in USDC.** No physical delivery of the underlying asset occurs. At expiration, the difference between the strike price and the settlement price is paid out in USDC.
- **European-style exercise.** Options can only be exercised at expiration, not before. This simplifies pricing and removes the complexity of early exercise decisions.
- **Automatic exercise at expiry.** Any option that is in-the-money at expiration is automatically exercised. You do not need to manually exercise profitable contracts.

These design choices reduce operational complexity and make options accessible to a wider range of traders, while maintaining the full strategic flexibility that options provide.
